📚 SDR & Radio Glossary

ADC — Analog-to-Digital Converter. Converts analog radio signals into digital data a computer can process.
ADS-B — Automatic Dependent Surveillance–Broadcast. System used by aircraft to broadcast their position. Can be received with RTL-SDR.
AM — Amplitude Modulation. A method of encoding information by varying the strength (amplitude) of a radio wave.
Antenna — A device that sends or receives radio waves. Its size and shape determine which frequencies it works best with.
Bandwidth — The range of frequencies a signal occupies. Wider bandwidth = more data.
dB (Decibel) — A unit measuring signal strength. Higher dB = stronger signal.
Demodulation — The process of extracting the original message from a modulated radio wave.
Dipole Antenna — A simple antenna made of two equal-length wires. Good for general reception.
DSP — Digital Signal Processing. Using math and software to analyze and modify signals.
EM Spectrum — Electromagnetic Spectrum. The full range of electromagnetic radiation, from radio waves to gamma rays.
ESP32 — A low-cost microcontroller with Wi-Fi and Bluetooth, often used in IoT projects.
FFT — Fast Fourier Transform. A mathematical method to show which frequencies are present in a signal. Creates the spectrum display.
FM — Frequency Modulation. A method of encoding information by varying the frequency of a radio wave. Used by FM radio stations.
Frequency — How many times a wave cycles per second, measured in Hertz (Hz). Higher frequency = faster wave.
Gain — Amplification of a signal. More gain = louder signal, but also more noise.
GNU Radio — Free, open-source software for SDR signal processing. Uses visual flowgraphs.
HackRF One — An open-source SDR that can both transmit and receive, covering 1 MHz to 6 GHz.
Half-Duplex — Can transmit OR receive, but not both at the same time. HackRF One is half-duplex.
Hz (Hertz) — Unit of frequency. 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second. kHz = thousands, MHz = millions, GHz = billions.
I/Q Data — In-phase / Quadrature. The raw data format used by SDR receivers to capture radio signals.
IoT — Internet of Things. Everyday devices connected via wireless communication.
ISM Band — Industrial, Scientific, Medical. Radio frequencies available for general use without a license (e.g., 433 MHz, 2.4 GHz).
LoRa — Long Range. A radio technology for sending small amounts of data over very long distances (1-10+ km).
micro:bit — A small educational computer with a built-in 2.4 GHz radio, perfect for kids' first radio experiments.
Modulation — The process of putting a message onto a radio wave (a carrier signal).
NOAA — National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration. Their weather satellites broadcast images that can be received with SDR.
Noise Floor — The level of background radio noise. Signals must be stronger than the noise floor to be detected.
Nyquist Theorem — To correctly capture a signal, you must sample at least twice its frequency.
RSSI — Received Signal Strength Indicator. A measurement of how strong a received signal is.
RTL-SDR — A low-cost USB dongle that receives radio signals from 24 MHz to 1.7 GHz. Receive-only.
RX — Receive / Receiver.
Sample Rate — How many times per second the SDR measures the signal. Higher = more detail.
SDR — Software Defined Radio. A radio where traditional hardware is replaced by software on a computer.
Spectrum — A visual display showing signal strength across different frequencies.
Spreading Factor (SF) — In LoRa, controls the trade-off between range and data speed. Higher SF = longer range, slower speed.
SWR — Standing Wave Ratio. Measures how well an antenna is matched to its cable. Lower is better.
TX — Transmit / Transmitter.
UHF — Ultra High Frequency. 300 MHz to 3 GHz.
VHF — Very High Frequency. 30 MHz to 300 MHz.
Waterfall — A scrolling display that shows signals over time. Frequency on x-axis, time on y-axis, color = strength.
Wavelength — The physical length of one wave cycle. Longer wavelength = lower frequency.
